Regression to the Mean

Why extreme scores tend to move toward the average on retest — a consequence of measurement error, not real change

Students take the same test twice. The test-retest reliability slider controls how much random error affects scores. At low reliability, extreme Time-1 scorers regress strongly toward the mean at Time-2 — not because they changed, but because their extreme score partly reflected luck. Highlight the top or bottom performers to see the regression effect in action.

Expected regression: —
The diagonal dashed line is the identity (T1 = T2). The solid line is the OLS regression of T2 on T1 — its slope equals the test-retest reliability ρ. When ρ < 1, the regression line is less steep than the identity. This means: if you scored 2 SD above the mean at T1, your expected T2 score is only 2ρ SD above the mean.
Real-world consequences: Top scorers who receive an intervention appear to "improve" relative to the control group, and bottom scorers appear to "improve" after treatment — even if the treatment does nothing. Without a control group, regression to the mean masquerades as a treatment effect. Always include a control group.
